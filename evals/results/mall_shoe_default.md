The mall went quiet the way old buildings do—by degrees. First the shutters, then the music, then the last footsteps of the cleaning crew.

Marcus walked his usual route with the flashlight low. Food court was the last stop. He’d been night-shift six years. You learned the sounds that belonged.

The shoe did not belong. Red, cartoon dinosaur, Velcro half torn. Small. Maybe a four-year-old’s. He radioed. Static. Lost and found was a locked closet. Procedure said bag it, tag it, log it.

He stood with the shoe longer than procedure allowed. Kids got left in bathrooms. He’d sat through training videos. A single shoe could mean nothing. Or not.

He turned the lights back on, swept tables, checked the family restroom twice. Cameras showed a green coat and a dinosaur shirt around 8:40. No panic.

He called the police, unlocked the east entrance, and waited. Two days later the matching shoe turned up in a thrift donation three towns over. The boy was fine. The mother was embarrassed. Marcus got a write-up for caring first. On his next shift he still checked under the tables.
