I’m writing this from the Tuesday shift log, not because anyone asked, but because the left sneaker is still on the lost-and-found shelf and my hands still remember the grit in the tread.

At 1:14 a.m. Eastbridge Galleria was mostly papered glass and dollar pizza. Marcus Hale — me — cut the food court with a flashlight. Neon from the pretzel stand hummed like a cheap radio. Under table C7: toddler size 11, blue mesh, Velcro half peeled, name on the tongue half rubbed to a black J.

Channel 3 said no kid tickets. My throat stayed tight anyway. Fryer oil and lemon cleaner. Cold tile through the knee of my uniform when I crouched. I walked the loading dock, the bus stop that dies at ten, the cart with one frozen wheel behind the furniture outlet. Nothing. No second shoe.

I almost wrote *possible missing minor*. I bagged it instead: CHILD SHOE / FC C7 / 1:14 AM / M. HALE. Procedure. Paperwork over imagination. That was the choice I told myself was duty.

Here’s the turn I didn’t expect until Thursday at 6:40 p.m., when Anita waved me over before my shift. A woman in a green coat had come back with a boy in a dinosaur shirt and one blue sock. They’d left early Tuesday after a fight in the car; the shoe kicked free under C7; she’d been too angry to notice until the daycare called about “only one sneaker.” The boy was fine. He wanted the pretzel stand open. It wasn’t.

I took the bag off the shelf. The grit was still there. She laughed once, the embarrassed kind, and said she’d been replaying the night like a bad song stuck between stations — half a local jingle, half the argument. I signed the release. On the log I added a second line I never write: *item returned to guardian 6:42 PM Thu*.

Aftermath isn’t sirens. It’s the quiet hour after, when the mall is empty again and my shoulders finally drop, and I keep checking C7 out of habit like a scar you touch without meaning to. I used to think the job was catching the worst case. Turns out a lot of nights are just living with the shape of not knowing until the story walks back in wearing one sock.

I’m leaving this in the binder under July. If the next guard finds a shoe, maybe they’ll still call. Or maybe they’ll bag it and wait, the way people wait on half-finished songs. I don’t know which is wiser. I know the shelf looks emptier now, and that emptier is a kind of ending.
