# C7

Around 1:10 a.m. the food court still smelled like fryer oil and the chemical they sprayed on the tiles after nine. Pretzel neon stayed on because Facilities never got the work order. Under table C7, half in the sticky dark under the seat rail, a blue toddler sneaker: left foot, Velcro half peeled, grit in the tread, a name on the tongue rubbed nearly black.

I almost stepped on it.

Channel 3 was quiet. No kid tickets all shift. I bagged the shoe, wrote the time on a strip of masking tape, put it on the lost-and-found shelf next to a pair of glasses nobody claimed and a charger that had been there since June. Tag: CHILD SHOE / FC C7 / 1:14 AM / M. HALE.

I did not call the non-emergency line.

I walked the dock. Puddle by the dumpster. One cart that only rolled in circles. Bus stop after ten was empty except for a paper cup stuck under the bench. I came back and almost wrote *possible missing minor* on the log. Left the line clean.

That is the part I still revise in my head.

---

Three years earlier, in a different mall two exits south, I was the one waiting under a clock that ran four minutes fast. My daughter was six. Blue coat. She had gone to the restroom and not come back in the time I thought she should. The security kid who found her by the fountain looked about nineteen and was shaking. She had been watching the koi. She was fine. I was not. I bought her a soft pretzel and sat on a bench until my hands stopped doing the thing.

I do not put that in the report either.

---

Eastbridge Mall is dying in the ordinary way. Half the second floor is papered with *Coming Soon* that never comes. The carousel runs on weekends for birthday parties that book the cheap package. The owner lives in Singapore; we get emails through a management company in Houston. Night crew is me, Rosa on cameras until two, and a rotating third who mostly checks their phone by the north entrance.

Rosa buzzed me at 1:22.

"You bag something blue?"

"Shoe. Kid size."

"Want me to scan the last hour?"

"If you have it."

She had it. Food court cams are old. Grainy. Between 12:40 and 1:05 a woman in a dark coat moves through with a stroller, stops near C7, digs for something, leaves. No clear face. No second adult. At 12:58 a cleaner rolls past and does not look down. At 1:11 I enter frame and nearly kick the sneaker.

"You calling it in?" Rosa asked.

"No ticket," I said. "No second shoe. Probably fell out of a bag."

"Or," she said, and did not finish.

I said I would walk it again. I did. Dock. Bus. Parking structure level P1 where the lights flicker in a pattern that used to bother me and now is just weather. Nothing.

On the shelf the shoe looked smaller.

---

Thursday, 6:40 p.m., I was early for a shift swap. Daylight crowd: teens, two old men arguing about parking validation, a woman in a green coat near Guest Services with a boy on her hip, one sock.

Anita from Guest Services waved me over like we were friends. We are not. We nod.

"This is Mrs. Cole," Anita said. "She's looking for a shoe."

The boy stared at my radio. One sock was grey. The bare foot was cold-looking under mall air.

Mrs. Cole did not cry. She spoke like she had already practiced for the police and then decided we were not the police.

"We left Tuesday. Fight in the car. I thought his bag had both. Daycare called yesterday. I checked under the seats. I checked the house. I thought maybe here."

I got the bag from the shelf. She looked at the tongue, the black smear where a name had been, and her mouth did a small hard thing.

"J," she said. "Jonah."

She did not thank me in a speech. She said "okay" twice and signed the log with a pen that clicked every time she set it down. Jonah put the shoe on sitting on the Guest Services carpet, Velcro loud in the quiet.

I almost said I had walked the dock. I almost said I had not called. I said nothing useful.

After they left, Anita said, "Lucky."

I said, "Yeah."

---

Lucky is a word that papers over a gap.

Between Tuesday night and Thursday evening the shoe sat on a shelf next to glasses. Anyone with a key to the cage could have taken it. Staff. A cleaner. A bored kid on a dare. The camera on that hallway has a blind corner; Rosa has put in three tickets about it. Houston has not answered.

I still do not know if Mrs. Cole would have preferred a call Tuesday night that turned into a police report and a story on the neighborhood app, or a quiet shelf and a Thursday recovery. I know what I chose because it was quieter for me.

That is not the same as knowing it was right.

---

The mall has a service corridor behind the food court where the pipes sweat. In winter you can see your breath. I eat there sometimes so I do not have to perform friendliness in the break room. There is a folding chair and a milk crate and a copy of a newspaper from 2019 that someone keeps refolding.

Two nights after the shoe left, I sat there with coffee that tasted like the urn had not been cleaned since the newspaper. My phone buzzed: a message from my ex, short, about dental insurance for our daughter. I answered with a thumbs-up and hated myself for the thumbs-up. Then I put the phone face down and listened to the HVAC do its long mechanical inhale.

I used to think night work was empty time. It is not empty. It is full of other people's unfinished objects. Shoes. Glasses. Chargers. Arguments paused mid-sentence when someone walks into the light.

---

Flash forward is the wrong term. There is no cinematic wipe. There is only later.

Later, the carousel contractor went unpaid and the horses froze mid-prance with a tarp over the ticket booth. Later, Rosa got a day job at a hospital parking garage and trained her replacement in two shifts. Later, I stayed, which is either loyalty or inertia depending on who asks.

Jonah Cole does not come back into this story as a character with arcs. I saw a green coat once in the parking lot six months after and my stomach did a stupid lift and it was not them.

I still check under C7 when I pass. Habit more than hope. The tile there is sticky again, same as always. Sometimes there is a straw wrapper. Once there was a glove. Never another shoe in that size.

---

If you want the Tuesday night I did not call, here it is without polish.

I held the radio. I thought about the report. I thought about the teenager by the fountain three years ago and my daughter and the pretzel salt on my fingers. I thought about Houston and tickets and whether a single left sneaker is enough to light up three cruisers. I thought about Mrs. Cole, who I did not know yet, fighting in a car, and I made the small cowardly calculation that most nights end without a tragedy and that I wanted to be in that majority.

I put the radio down.

Rosa did not push. She is not paid to push. The log stayed clean.

---

There is a subplot I keep leaving out because it does not resolve.

On Monday of that same week—before the shoe—someone keyed six cars in P2. We pulled footage. The person wore a hoodie and walked like they knew the camera angles. Management sent a memo about "heightened awareness." The insurance emails went to Houston. No arrest. The memo is still on the break room fridge under a magnet shaped like a pretzel.

I mention it because the week was already a pile of partial things. The shoe was not a clean incident with a beginning and a moral. It was one more object in a system that loses objects for a living.

---

Mrs. Cole's signature is still in the book. Blue pen. The click pen. Anita keeps the book under the counter with the gift card envelopes and the map of stores that no longer exist. I looked at the page once on a slow Sunday. The handwriting slopes up. Jonah's name is not written; only "shoe returned to parent."

I did not write my almost-line: *possible missing minor.*

If I had written it, someone would have had to call. If someone had called, maybe Mrs. Cole would have slept worse Tuesday and better Thursday, or the reverse. Counterfactuals are free and worthless. I know what the page says.

---

Night work teaches you the building's noises until they are language. The dock door chain. The ice machine behind the closed Chinese place. The escalator that runs empty after ten with a squeak on the third step from the bottom. You start to time your rounds by them.

On a night in February, months after, the squeak stopped. Maintenance had oiled it without a ticket. I stood at the bottom for a full minute waiting for a sound that was no longer there, feeling stupid, then walked on.

Loss is not always a person. Sometimes it is a squeak.

---

I am not going to tell you what the shoe meant.

I am going to tell you that on the Thursday Mrs. Cole took it, I went back to C7 after my break and stood under the pretzel neon and looked at the gap on the shelf from across the court like the gap was a tooth. I left it. Shelves are allowed to have gaps. People are allowed to leave without a scene.

Driving home at 7:05 a.m., Route 17 already thick, I passed the exit for the mall two exits south—the one with the fountain and the koi—and did not take it. My daughter is nine now. She texts in fragments. She does not remember the pretzel the way I do. That is fine. Memory is not evenly distributed.

At the apartment I put my keys in the bowl and washed my hands longer than soap required. The blue of the sneaker stayed in my head like a color you see after a camera flash. Not a lesson. A residue.

---

Rosa, before she left for the hospital garage, said something I only half heard because a delivery was buzzing the dock.

"You ever think we keep the lost stuff so the building feels less empty?"

I said maybe. She laughed like I had missed the joke. Maybe I had. Maybe she was talking about herself. People do that at 2 a.m.

---

There was a second almost-call, which is the kind of detail that ruins a clean story, so here it is.

Wednesday night—shoe still on the shelf—I nearly dialed non-emergency after a woman reported a "weird feeling" near the restrooms. Weird feelings are the mall's primary product after ten. I walked her to her car. Nothing. In the lot I looked at my phone and thought about the blue sneaker two floors up and the clean log line and whether stacking two nothings made a something. I put the phone away. Wednesday ended.

Thursday the shoe left with a boy and a green coat.

If you need a bow: there is none. The cars in P2 are still keyed in the insurance file. The blind corner still blinds. Houston still owes Rosa three camera tickets. Jonah is somewhere with two shoes on a good day and one sock on a bad one. I still work nights.

---

Once, years before Eastbridge, I read a paperback in a break room about a detective who always found the missing child in the last chapter. The pages smelled like the previous guard's cologne. I did not finish it. Someone took it before my next shift. I hope they finished it. I hope the last chapter was satisfying for them.

This is not that book.

---

Inventory of what I know for sure:

The shoe was real. The time was about 1:14 a.m. The tag was my handwriting. Rosa saw grainy stroller footage. Mrs. Cole signed. Jonah put the shoe on the carpet. The dock cart still only rolls in circles. The pretzel neon is still on. My daughter still hates the phone for long calls. I still do not call every time something looks like it might become a story.

Inventory of what I do not know:

Whether the woman with the stroller was Mrs. Cole. Whether the fight in the car was loud enough for the boy to remember. Whether a call Tuesday would have been care or theater. Whether "lucky" is Anita's kindness or her dismissal. Whether I am a careful guard or a man who prefers clean logs.

I can live with a list that does not balance.

---

On a wet Tuesday this spring—different Tuesday—I found a glove under C7. Adult. Black. Leather worn white at the palm. I bagged it. I tagged it. I put it on the shelf. I did not call anyone. I walked the dock out of habit. Nothing. The glove is still there as of last night, unless someone claimed it while I was on P2 chasing a door alarm that turned out to be wind.

I am writing this down because the shoe keeps arriving in my head when I am trying to sleep in daylight, which is the occupational hazard. Not guilt exactly. Not pride. The sticky tile. The almost-step. The radio in my hand. The clean line.

If there is a second shoe somewhere in a car seat crack or a daycare cubby or a trash bag from Tuesday's fight, I will never match it. Pairing is a luxury of closed cases.

---

Eastbridge will close someday. The Houston emails already sound like pre-obituaries. When it closes, the lost-and-found will be boxed, and someone will throw away the glasses and the charger and maybe the glove, and the log book will go in a dumpster with Anita's pen. Mrs. Cole's signature will become landfill. That is not tragedy. That is how malls end.

I might apply to the hospital garage where Rosa went. I might not. Night is a hard habit to break once your body learns the wrong daylight.

---

Last thing, without a moral:

I still glance under C7. The gap on the shelf is ordinary. Ordinary is the point. Most missing shoes are not missing children. Most clean log lines are fine. Most almost-calls are correct.

Some are not.

I do not get to know which night I am in until later, and later is unevenly distributed too.

The pretzel neon hums. The cart circles. Somewhere Jonah is older by the width of a few months. Somewhere a stroller rolls through another food court under worse cameras. I clock out. I drive Route 17. I do not take the exit with the koi.

Home, keys in the bowl, hands washed, residue of blue, no lesson I would trust if someone printed it on a poster in the break room under the pretzel magnet.

That is the whole of it, or as much as fits before the sun comes up and the day crew unlocks the glass and pretends the night did not happen.
