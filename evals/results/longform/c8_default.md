# The Unspeakable Miracle: A Brief Appreciation of Poop

Nobody wants to talk about it, and yet everybody does it. The average human produces roughly 360 pounds of feces per year, which means that over a lifetime, you will personally manufacture somewhere between eleven and thirteen tons of the stuff — approximately the weight of two adult elephants. You are, among other things, a poop factory of impressive industrial capacity, and you've probably never once stopped to admire the operation.

Let's fix that.

## What It Actually Is

Here's something that surprises most people: poop is not really food. Or rather, only a small fraction of it is. The popular image — that feces are simply the indigestible remains of what you ate, compacted and expelled — is mostly wrong. By weight, a healthy stool is about 75 percent water. Of the remaining solid matter, roughly a third consists of bacteria. Living and dead microbes, trillions of them, the citizens of your gut microbiome, escorted out of the body in enormous daily migrations. Another third is fiber and other indigestible plant material. The final third is a fascinating slurry of things your body wanted gone: dead cells shed from the intestinal lining, mucus, fats, proteins, and — critically — the breakdown products of old red blood cells.

That last part explains the color. When red blood cells die after their roughly 120-day lifespan, your liver recycles their hemoglobin into a pigment called bilirubin, which gets further processed into stercobilin. Stercobilin is brown. Your poop is brown, in other words, because it's full of the rust of your own blood — a detail that is either disgusting or poetically beautiful, depending on your temperament. Bile, which starts out greenish-yellow, contributes too, which is why digestive transit time affects the shade: fast transit, greener; slow transit, darker brown.

The smell, for those keeping score, comes largely from skatole and indole, two sulfur-adjacent compounds produced by bacterial fermentation of amino acids. Here's the wonderful part: skatole in low concentrations smells faintly floral, and it is genuinely used in perfumery. In small doses it appears in orange blossom and jasmine fragrances. The same molecule that clears a room is, diluted enough, dabbed behind ears. Chemistry has a sense of humor.

## The Bristol Scale and Other Medical Elegance

Doctors, being practical people, needed a way to discuss poop without giggling or gagging, so in 1997 researchers at the University of Bristol developed the Bristol Stool Scale, a seven-point classification system ranging from Type 1 (separate hard lumps, like nuts — you're constipated) to Type 7 (entirely liquid — you have diarrhea). Type 4 — smooth, soft, sausage- or snake-like — is the gold standard, the stool against which all other stools are judged. Gastroenterologists will ask patients about it with a completely straight face, which is one of the great unheralded achievements of medical professionalism.

This isn't trivia. Your bowel movements are a remarkably honest diagnostic instrument. Blood in the stool can signal anything from hemorrhoids to colon cancer. Pale, clay-colored stools suggest bile duct problems. Black, tarry stools can indicate bleeding in the upper gastrointestinal tract. Sudden persistent changes in shape or habit warrant a doctor's visit. Your body files a written report every single day, and most of us flush it unread.

## The Gut-Brain Conversation

Modern science has spent the last two decades discovering that the contents of your colon are in active conversation with your brain. The gut contains around 100 million neurons — the "second brain," formally the enteric nervous system — and gut bacteria produce a startling share of the body's serotonin, along with dopamine precursors and other neuroactive compounds. Fecal matter, viewed this way, isn't waste; it's a status update from an organ that participates in your mood, your immune system, and possibly your risk of depression, obesity, and autoimmune disease.

The most dramatic proof of the microbiome's importance is the fecal microbiota transplant — yes, exactly what it sounds like. A healthy donor's stool, processed and screened, is transferred into a sick patient's gut. For recurrent Clostridioides difficile infection, a brutal and sometimes fatal condition that antibiotics often fail to cure, fecal transplants achieve cure rates above 85 percent. It is one of the most effective treatments in all of modern medicine, and it is literally made of poop. Medicine spent centuries trying to sanitize everything, and the breakthrough turned out to be giving people more bacteria, delivered by the least glamorous route imaginable.

## A Long and Useful History

Humans' relationship with dung extends far beyond our own bodies. For most of agricultural history, manure was wealth. Farmers didn't waste a dropping; animal feces fertilized the fields that fed civilization. In many parts of the world, dried dung — cattle, buffalo, camel — has served for millennia as cooking fuel and building material. Dung cakes still heat millions of homes. The word "guano" entered economics as a geopolitical force: in the 19th century, nations fought literal wars over islands covered in centuries of seabird droppings, so valuable was the nitrogen-rich fertilizer. Peru's guano boom funded railways and reshaped its economy. The United States passed the Guano Islands Act of 1856, allowing citizens to claim unoccupied guano-covered islands for the country. We have, as a species, invaded territory for poop.

Nature, of course, was way ahead of us. Dung beetles perform one of the planet's essential sanitation services, burying and consuming feces and recycling nutrients back into soil. Some species navigate by the Milky Way while rolling their dung balls — the only insects known to use the galaxy for orientation, which means that somewhere right now a beetle is steering a sphere of excrement by starlight, and honestly that's one of the most beautiful facts in biology. Elephants produce dung so fibrous it can be made into paper. Coffee beans passed through the digestive tract of civets become kopi luwak, among the most expensive coffee on Earth. Pandas, being inefficient digesters of bamboo, produce droppings mild enough that Chinese entrepreneurs have turned them into souvenir products.

## The Point

Here's the honest thesis of this essay: the universal disgust surrounding feces is both understandable and slightly ridiculous. Understandable, because disgust is a disease-avoidance system, and feces genuinely can carry pathogens — the taboo has saved countless lives. Ridiculous, because the same material is a window into your health, a pillar of agriculture, a medical treatment, a historical commodity worth wars, an ecological keystone, and a daily reminder that you are not a sealed, self-contained being but a walking ecosystem carrying more bacterial cells than human ones.

Every organism on Earth with an anus participates. Every animal that ever lived did. It is the most democratic of all biological processes — popes and presidents, whales and beetles, your ancestors ten thousand years back, everyone. We've built whole industries around pretending it doesn't happen, with scented sprays and euphemisms and embarrassed silences, when the truth is that the daily bowel movement is one of the quiet triumphs of being alive: proof the machinery works, the gut is fed, the microbes are thriving, and the body, that astonishing and unglamorous engine, is doing exactly what it was built to do.

So perhaps, just once, before you flush, take a moment. Not a long one. Just a nod of respect for the two elephants you'll eventually leave behind.
