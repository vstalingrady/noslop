On Poop

Say the word to a five-year-old and you get the best laugh in the world. Say it at a dinner party and the room does something complicated: a few people grin, a few look at their plates, somebody changes the subject to the weather. Poop is the rare subject that can be hilarious, disgusting, or boring, and which one of those it is depends entirely on your age and your company.

Nobody has fully explained the taboo. The smell is part of it, and germs presumably; disgust is an old immune-system reflex, and it fires hardest at bodily waste. But disgust explains why we avoid the stuff, not why we laugh about it. The laugh is doing some other job, and I don't know what that job is, and as far as I can tell neither does anyone else.

Here is what it actually is. Mostly water. Of the solid part, a large share, by some estimates close to half, is bacteria, most of them already dead: fallout from the fermentation vat you carry around in your abdomen. The rest is fiber your body couldn't use, cells shed from the lining of your gut, and the chemical remains of old blood. The brown color comes from bilirubin, a pigment your liver pulls out of worn-out red blood cells, which gut bacteria then convert into a compound called stercobilin. Your stool is, in a modest way, a blood report.

And strictly speaking, it was never inside you. Topologically, you are a donut. The tube running from mouth to anus is outside the body's interior the whole way; the gut wall is a border post deciding what gets admitted from the world. Poop is the freight that never cleared customs.

Everybody ships it, of course. In 1977 the Japanese author Taro Gomi published a children's book called Everyone Poops, which states its entire thesis in the title and then demonstrates it across elephants, camels, and one small boy. It has stayed in print ever since, because the thesis needs re-proving to every new cohort of three-year-olds. Kings do it. Astronauts do it, in orbit, with equipment. The pope does it. There has never been a human exception.

The strange thing is the silence. People will describe chest pain to a doctor, or a lump, or blood in their urine. They will not bring up their bowel habits, and so colon cancer, which is one of the more treatable cancers when found early, gets found late out of plain embarrassment. Gastroenterology even has an instrument for this, the Bristol stool scale, seven types from pellets to soup, worked out at the University of Bristol in the 1990s. Nurses genuinely use it, because the alternative, asking patients to describe things, produces nothing useful.

For most of human history the question was what to do with it, and the answers were all bad. In old Edinburgh, the story goes, you emptied the chamber pot out the window with a shout of "gardyloo," a corruption of the French for "watch the water." London employed night soil men to haul the stuff away by cart, and for a long time this worked because the stuff had value: it went to the fields as manure, and manure was wealth. Then the city outgrew the cart. In the hot summer of 1858 the Thames, into which London's sewers had been emptying for years, produced what the papers called the Great Stink, and Parliament, sitting in riverside rooms with curtains soaked in chloride of lime, finally voted the money for Joseph Bazalgette's sewer network, which is still under London today. Cholera had already taught the sharper lesson: in 1854 John Snow traced an outbreak to a single water pump on Broad Street and had the handle taken off. The flush toilet, meanwhile, had existed since 1596, when John Harington built one for his godmother, Queen Elizabeth I. She used it and declined to make it fashionable. Thomas Crapper did not invent the thing, whatever you heard; he was a Victorian plumber with a gift for publicity and an excellent surname.

Step outside the house and none of it goes to waste. Dung beetles reach a fresh pile within minutes and start carving it into balls, rolling them off to eat or to lay eggs in. One species, Scarabaeus satyrus, works at night and keeps its ball straight by steering against the Milky Way, the only insect known to navigate by the galaxy. In the nineteenth century, bird droppings were a strategic material. Peruvian guano, centuries of compressed seabird poop, was the best fertilizer on earth before synthetic nitrogen existed; the United States passed the Guano Islands Act of 1856 letting its citizens claim uninhabited islands for the droppings on them, and dozens of islands were claimed. Spain and Peru fought a war in the 1860s that was substantially about bird poop.

The beetles do not find any of this awkward.

Current science says the gut's bacterial crowd talks to the immune system and possibly to the brain, and the wellness industry has already begun selling test kits and fermented everything on the strength of that "possibly." How much of it holds up, nobody knows yet.

Meanwhile the daily work goes on. Every evening in my neighborhood the dog walkers come out, each carrying a small plastic bag, each performing the same unglamorous civic duty for an animal that has no opinion on the matter. Nobody laughs. The dogs look mildly proud.
