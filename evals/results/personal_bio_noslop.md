I’m Dina Sari, 29, writing this from a shared desk in Tebet at 7:10 a.m. before standup — coffee too hot, left shoulder still tight from last night’s badminton at GOR Ragunan.

Three years ago I was on-call for a payments team that measured worth in ticket velocity. I left the week my hands started shaking on the train home to Bekasi. Now I ship routing services for a logistics startup that moves chili and phone cases across Java; the on-call still exists, but the pages are fewer and the failures smell like diesel and late invoices, not abstract SLO poetry.

The turn — the climax — wasn’t a TED moment. It was one Sunday last March where I won two sets, lost one, and realized my neck had unclenched for the first time in months. That was the peak.

What came after is the part bios skip.

Monday after that Sunday: standup at 9:00. I still wrote Go. I still broke a staging route. But I slept in my own bed Sunday night, and my hands were steady on the train.

Two weeks later: another Sunday at GOR, same cheap grip tape, same bad jokes. Neck stayed loose through the third set. I put “court 4 p.m.” on the calendar as non-negotiable, the way on-call used to be.

A month out: I shipped a routing fix at 11:20 p.m., went home, and did not open Slack until morning. The failure still smelled like diesel. It did not smell like my worth.

Now, writing this at 7:10 in Tebet, the aftermath is still running — Sunday court, own bed, hands that mostly don’t shake. I still break prod sometimes. The second job that pays in sweat is what keeps the first one from owning my neck again.
