Rina checked the wall clock above the nurses' station: 03:17. The AC rattled. Outside, rain hammered the hospital's corrugated awning and the streets of Cempaka Putih had gone to a wet black shine.

Bed 12 was supposed to be Pak Hartono. Sixty-two. Chest pain, elevated troponin, pending second ECG. She'd hung a second bag of saline at 01:40 and written the dose in blue pen, the way she always did, because the printers jammed and nobody trusted the tablets after midnight.

The folder on the counter was not the folder she had left.

She flipped it open under the desk lamp. Diagnosis line: acute gastritis. Plan: discharge home, oral PPI, outpatient follow-up. Below that, a signature she half-recognized—Dr. Yusuf's loopy Y—timestamped 02:55. Vitals chart cleaned. The troponin strip gone. Her saline note overwritten in black: patient stable, refused admission.

Rina stood still long enough that her coffee went cold in her hand. Anger came first, sharp and useful. Then a thinner thing under it: fear of the kind that makes you check the door.

She walked to Bed 12.

Empty. Sheets folded with that tidy, institutional cruelty. The IV pole pushed against the wall. On the nightstand, a paper cup of half-drunk Teh Botol and a bus ticket stub for Rawasari–Senen, 05:00.

"Where's Hartono?" she asked Sari at the desk.

Sari didn't look up from the inventory sheet. "Discharged. Yusuf signed. Family took him. Or a cousin. Someone."

"He had a troponin of 1.8 at midnight."

"Chart says gastritis."

"I wrote the chart."

Sari finally looked at her. Tired eyes, lipstick worn off. "Then rewrite less carefully next time. Or sleep through it. I'm not arguing with paper at three in the morning."

Rina went to the med room and pulled the waste bag from the morning shift's bin. Under gauze wrappers and an empty ampoule of ondansetron she found the printout she'd torn earlier—smudged, folded twice. Troponin I: 1.84 ng/mL. 00:12. Her initials in the corner.

She photocopied it on the old machine that smelled of warm plastic, folded the copy into her scrub pocket, and put the original in an envelope addressed to no one yet.

At 04:02 the on-call phone rang. Yusuf's voice, thin, almost casual.

"Rina. If anyone asks about Bed 12, you saw a discharge. Understood?"

She watched rain crawl down the window. "I saw a man with a heart attack leave with a bus ticket."

Silence. Then: "You saw gastritis. Don't make me write you up for documentation error. You're good at your job. Stay good."

He hung up.

She sat at the station and opened a blank incident form. Cursor blinking. The hospital logo at the top looked too clean for what the night had become. She typed two lines, deleted them. Typed again: Patient chart altered between 01:40 and 03:17. Original labs attached. Her hand shook once, then steadied.

Outside, a bajaj coughed past. The 05:00 buses would start soon. Pak Hartono might already be on one, chest tight, someone else's signature in his pocket like a ticket he didn't buy.

Rina printed the form, signed it, and slid the envelope under the night supervisor's door—knowing the supervisor would open it at seven, knowing seven was a different country from three, knowing she might still be wrong about what "cousin" meant.

She clocked her next round at 04:30. Beds 8 through 11. Fluids, pain scores, a child with dengue whose mother slept upright in a plastic chair.

Bed 12 stayed empty. The Teh Botol stayed where it was. She left it.
