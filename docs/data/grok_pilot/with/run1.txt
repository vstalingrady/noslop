Rina checked the wall clock: 03:17. AC rattled. Rain hit the corrugated awning over Cempaka Putih and the street went black-shiny.

Bed 12 was Pak Hartono. Sixty-two. Chest pain, troponin up, second ECG pending. She'd hung saline at 01:40 in blue pen because the printers jammed after midnight.

The folder on the counter was not the one she left.

Under the desk lamp: acute gastritis. Discharge, oral PPI, outpatient follow-up. Dr. Yusuf's loopy Y at 02:55. Troponin strip gone. Her saline note overwritten: patient stable, refused admission.

Anger first. Then fear that made her check the door.

Bed 12 empty. Sheets folded. IV pole against the wall. Half-drunk Teh Botol. Bus stub Rawasari–Senen, 05:00.

"Where's Hartono?" she asked Sari.

"Discharged. Yusuf signed. Family. Or a cousin."

"He had a troponin of 1.8."

"Chart says gastritis."

In the med-room trash, under gauze and an ondansetron ampoule, she found her printout: Troponin I 1.84 ng/mL, 00:12, her initials. She photocopied it. Pocketed the copy. Envelope for later.

At 04:02 Yusuf called. Thin voice. "If anyone asks about Bed 12, you saw a discharge."

"I saw a man with a heart attack leave with a bus ticket."

"You saw gastritis. Don't make me write you up."

He hung up.

She typed an incident form, deleted it, typed again: chart altered between 01:40 and 03:17, labs attached. Hand shook once. Steady. Slid the envelope under the night supervisor's door — knowing seven was a different country from three.

04:30 rounds. Beds 8–11. Dengue kid, mother asleep in a plastic chair. Bed 12 stayed empty. She left the Teh Botol where it was.
