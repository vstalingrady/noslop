The ampoule rolled when she tipped the bag. Ondansetron. Empty. Under it, the folded strip: Troponin I 1.84 ng/mL, 00:12.

Rina's initials in the corner. She had torn that print hours ago and forgotten it in the rush.

Chart on the counter said gastritis and home. Yusuf at 02:55. Bed 12 bare as a closed shop.

Sari: discharged. Cousin, maybe.

Rina walked the length of the ward once. Printers still jammed. Wall clock 03:41. She photocopied the strip, smelled warm plastic, put the copy in her scrub pocket where the scissors live.

Phone. Yusuf.

Silence after her sentence about the bus ticket.

Then: write-up. Stay good. Click.

A bajaj coughed past on wet asphalt. Five o'clock buses would start. Hartono might already be sitting with someone's signature in his pocket like a ticket he did not buy.

She printed the short incident form. Two lines. Signature. Envelope under the supervisor door — knowing mornings rewrite nights.

Next round: dengue kid, mother in a plastic chair, upright, mouth open. Rina checked the line, the urine bag, the forehead.

When she passed Bay 12 again, the Teh Botol was still there. Condensation gone flat. She left it.

Somewhere toward Senen, a bus would take on one more passenger who should have stayed for an ECG.
