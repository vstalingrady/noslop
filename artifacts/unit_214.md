# Unit 214

The motion light over bay C clicked on at 1:07 a.m. and stayed on long enough that Leah noticed it from the office window. Most nights the lights did that—raccoons, wind, a plastic bag catching a camera angle. She waited for it to die. It did not die. It pulsed once more when something moved between the rows, then held.

Harbor Self-Storage sat on a frontage road behind a closed carpet warehouse and a place that sold used tires until the owner moved to Arizona. The sign still said HARBOR even though the nearest water was a drainage ditch that smelled like oil when it rained. Leah worked nights. She preferred them. Day people wanted receipts explained. Night people mostly wanted the gate code to work.

She wrote 1:07 on a sticky note, then 1:12 when the light finally went out. The camera feed for bay C was a grey smear. The company had upgraded the front gate cameras two years ago and left the back rows alone. Management in a different city sent emails about “situational awareness.” Leah kept a flashlight in the drawer with the rubber bands and a half-empty bottle of ibuprofen.

At 1:40 she walked it. Jacket on. Master keys on the ring that cut her hip when she sat wrong. The asphalt still held heat from the day. Unit doors ran in dull orange rows, roll-ups locked, some with padlocks that had rusted into sculpture. 214 was in the middle of C, same as always: a standard ten-by-ten, lock on the hasp that was not one of Harbor’s rental locks. Customer lock. Blue body, scratched.

She put her palm near the metal. Warm from sun, not from people. She almost turned back.

The sticky note said 1:07. She hated unfinished lines.

She knocked once, soft, the way you knock on a door when you hope no one answers. Nothing. She knocked harder. From inside, a sound like fabric shifting. Not raccoon. Raccoons did not go still after.

“Harbor staff,” she said. Her voice sounded borrowed. “If someone’s in there, say so.”

Silence long enough to make her feel stupid. Then: “Okay.”

A kid’s voice. Not little. Teen. Trying not to shake and failing.

Leah’s hand went to her radio and stopped. Policy was clear. Unauthorized occupancy: call the day manager, call non-emergency if needed, do not open units alone after midnight. Policy had been written by someone who slept through 1 a.m.

“You hurt?” she asked.

“No.”

“You alone?”

A pause. “Yeah.”

She should have walked away and logged a note for morning. She should have. She put the master on the outer hasp—useless against the customer padlock—and said, “I’m going to the office. Don’t open that door unless I knock three times. If I see you in the lane on camera, I call.”

“Okay,” the voice said again, smaller.

Back in the office she sat with the gate software open and did not call. She drank coffee that had been on the burner since ten. She thought about her brother Mark, who had slept in a car for six weeks in 2019 and never told their mother until it was over. She thought about the camera grey smear and how it would not help anyone.

At 2:15 she knocked three times on 214.

The padlock came off from inside. The roll-up went up a foot, then two. A face in the gap: sixteen, maybe seventeen, hair cut uneven, hoodie with a school logo Leah did not recognize. Behind him, a mattress that had seen better decades, a backpack, a plastic grocery bag of something soft.

“Name,” Leah said.

“Jamie.”

“Last name.”

Jamie’s mouth closed.

“Fine. Jamie is fine. You can’t stay in a unit. Fire code. Also theft reports. Also I get fired.”

“I’m not stealing.”

“I didn’t say you were.”

They looked at each other in the sodium light. Somewhere on the frontage road a truck downshifted.

“You got family?” Leah asked.

“Not useful family.”

Leah almost laughed and did not. “Come to the office. Ten minutes. Then we figure something that is not this.”

Jamie hesitated, then rolled the door down and locked it like a person locking a house. That detail sat wrong in Leah’s chest. You do not lock a storage unit like a house unless it has become one.


## 2

The office smelled like dust and the lemon cleaner the day crew used on the counter. Leah poured water into a mug that said WORLD’S OKAYEST EMPLOYEE, a joke gift from a cousin. Jamie sat in the plastic chair reserved for people arguing about late fees.

“How long?” Leah asked.

“On and off. Two weeks here. Before that other places.”

“How’d you get the code?”

Jamie looked at the floor. “Someone’s gate code. From a post. People are dumb.”

“People are tired,” Leah said. “Don’t post codes. Also don’t use posted codes. Circle of life.”

Jamie almost smiled. The almost was enough.

Leah logged into the rental system and typed nothing useful. Unit 214 was rented to a D. Okonkwo, paid through month end, no flags. Day crew would open for access during hours. Jamie was a ghost on a paid square of concrete.

“You can’t stay,” Leah said again.

“I know.”

“So what’s the plan.”

Jamie shrugged with one shoulder. “Morning. Bus. Maybe a shelter downtown if they have beds. They didn’t yesterday.”

Leah’s phone sat face down. She could call her sister, who worked social services two counties over and would answer with a list. She could call Mark. She could do nothing and write a clean log.

She wrote on the sticky note under 1:07: *C214 — spoke w/ minor — no call yet.*

“You eat?” she asked.

“Not really.”

There were crackers in the break drawer and a protein bar that had expired in March. She gave Jamie both and watched them disappear without performance. When the bar wrapper went into the trash, Jamie flattened it first, neat.

“I’m not calling cops,” Leah said. “I’m also not running a hotel. You sleep here tonight—office floor, not the unit—and at six you leave before day shift. If I see 214 lit again, I don’t get to be nice twice.”

Jamie nodded. “Why.”

“Why what.”

“Why not call.”

Leah thought of Mark’s car and the way their mother had said *you could have told me* like the telling would have changed the rent. “Because the call has a life of its own,” she said. “And I’m tired of lives that get bigger than the person.”


## 3

Jamie slept poorly on the floor with Leah’s spare jacket as a pillow. Leah sat in the rolling chair and watched gate logs like television. At 4:50 a car idled at the keypad, failed three times, left. At 5:10 a regular—boat guy, unit A12—came for a cooler and waved at the camera the way people wave when they think it makes them look honest.

At 5:55 Leah shook Jamie’s shoulder. “Out.”

Jamie was up fast, backpack on, no argument. At the door Jamie said, “The mattress.”

“Leave it. Okonkwo’s problem if they notice. Or mine later.”

“It’s mine.”

“Then you move it at noon like a customer. Not now.”

Jamie’s jaw worked. Then: “Okay.”

The gate opened. The frontage road was the color of wet cardboard. Jamie walked toward the bus stop by the tire place without looking back. Leah watched until the hoodie became a dark mark and then nothing.

Day shift arrived at 6:02. Anita, who hummed, who knew every late-fee story by heart. “Quiet?” Anita asked.

“Quiet,” Leah said, and the sticky note went into her pocket instead of the log.


## 4

Three days of ordinary nights. No light on C. Leah almost convinced herself Jamie had found a couch. On the fourth night the motion log showed bay C at 12:48. She walked slower this time. 214’s padlock was the same blue. She knocked three times.

Jamie opened to the foot gap. “I know,” Jamie said before Leah spoke. “One night. Bus money’s short.”

“You said you wouldn’t.”

“I lied.”

Leah respected the honesty more than the promise. That was a problem.

“Office,” she said.

They did not talk much. Leah made instant oatmeal with water from the bathroom sink and microwave that sparked if you put metal near the edge. Jamie ate. Leah asked about school; Jamie said “not currently” like a phrase practiced for adults. Leah asked about D. Okonkwo; Jamie said the unit had been empty of real visits since June, that the mattress was from a thrift place, that the grocery bag was shirts.

“You can’t keep using someone else’s unit,” Leah said.

“I know.”

“Knowing isn’t stopping.”

Jamie looked at the WORLD’S OKAYEST mug. “You going to call.”

“Not tonight.”

“Tomorrow?”

“I don’t know.”

That was the true answer. She put it on the table between them like an object.


## 5

Saturday was Leah’s night off. She spent it at Mark’s apartment eating takeout that claimed to be Thai and was mostly sugar. Mark’s kid, June, was seven and building a Lego castle with no door. “How does anyone get in?” Leah asked.

“They live there already,” June said, serious.

Mark washed plates with the radio low. “You look like night shift,” he said.

“I am night shift.”

“Worse.”

Leah almost told him about 214. The sentence got as far as her teeth. She said instead, “Storage people leave weird stuff. Half a mannequin last month.”

Mark snorted. “Remember Dad’s garage?”

“Don’t.”

They remembered anyway: their father’s garage after the funeral, boxes labeled in a handwriting that assumed infinite time. Mark had taken the tools. Leah had taken a box of photographs and not opened it for a year. Their mother had taken nothing and then everything in a single weekend that left the house echoing.

June’s castle grew a wall where the door should have been. Leah helped stick a translucent blue brick in as a window. “Escape hatch,” she said.

June considered. “Okay. For emergencies.”


## 6

Monday, Anita pulled Leah aside before shift change. “Okonkwo called. Says someone messed with 214. Wants a camera review. Also wants a fee waived for the inconvenience.”

Leah’s stomach did a small cold thing. “What kind of messed.”

“Lock scratched. Door scuffed. Nothing missing they can name. They’re coming Tuesday at ten.”

“I’ll note it,” Leah said.

In the office alone she stood in front of the camera wall and rewound bay C until her eyes hurt. Grey, grey, a blur that could be a person or a bag. Harbor’s lawyers liked certainty. The feed offered none.

She texted a number she had made Jamie put in her phone the second night—Jamie’s, prepaid, low battery emoji in the profile blank.

*Okonkwo coming Tue 10. Clear 214 tonight. Mattress out. No arguments.*

Three dots. Then: *ok*

Then: *where*

Leah stared at the word. She was not a shelter. She was not family. She was a person with a master key and a bad habit of unfinished lines.

*Bus stop. 9pm. I’ll open the side gate. You haul. After that you’re not my problem on camera.*

Cruel on purpose. Clear on purpose.


## 7

Monday night Jamie moved like someone who had done this before—mattress on edge, awkward angle through the roll-up, backpack strangling one shoulder. Leah held the gate and watched the frontage road for headlights that would turn into questions. A moth hit the office window again and again, stupid and consistent.

When the mattress was on the sidewalk, Jamie sat on it as if waiting for a bus that used furniture.

“Shelter?” Leah asked.

“Full Friday. I’ll try.”

“You got a real name for forms.”

Jamie’s face closed. “Jamie is real enough.”

Leah went inside, came back with forty dollars she would call lunch for the week. “For the bus. Not for a unit.”

Jamie did not do gratitude theater. Took the money. Nodded. “You going to get in trouble.”

“Maybe.”

“Why help then.”

Leah thought of June’s doorless castle and Mark’s car year and the sticky note in her pocket that had gone through the wash and left a pulp. “Because I already did,” she said. “Stopping mid-mistake feels worse than finishing it wrong.”

Jamie left the mattress. Leah should have made them take it. She dragged it to the dumpster corral herself after midnight and hated the sound it made going in.


## 8

Tuesday at 10:05, D. Okonkwo was a woman in a clean jacket with a voice trained on phone trees. She wanted the fee waived. She wanted assurance. She wanted Leah to say the word *secure* like a spell. Leah said the unit was locked, the account current, the scuffs consistent with normal wear. She did not say *teenager*. She did not say *oatmeal*. She signed the clipboard where Anita pointed.

After Okonkwo left, Anita said, “You sure nothing happened?”

“I’m sure we don’t have video worth anything.”

Anita hummed, unconvinced, and went back to a couple arguing about a sofa that would not fit a ten-by-ten no matter how optimistically they angled it.


## 9

September turned the ditch greener and the nights cooler. Leah bought a better jacket. Jamie’s number went dark for two weeks. Leah told herself that was success. Success felt like a missing tooth—you keep checking the gap with your tongue.

On a Thursday she saw the hoodie at the grocery store three miles inland, not a Harbor regular’s path. Jamie was comparing prices on peanut butter. Leah almost walked to the dairy case. Instead she stood in the cereal aisle until Jamie paid cash and left with a small bag. No approach. No reunion scene. The automatic doors stuttered. Leah bought milk she did not need.


## 10

October brought a corporate memo about “wellness” and a new rule: any suspected overnight occupancy required an immediate call to a regional number that rang to a call center. Leah printed the memo and taped it inside the supply closet where only she would see it, which was not compliance so much as a private joke with a bitter aftertaste.

Rain came hard on a Sunday night. The roof over bay B ticked. A customer called the emergency line about a leak near their wine boxes; Leah walked it with a flashlight and a tarp and thought about how people store the expensive soft things and leave the cheap hard things in houses. When she returned, soaked at the cuffs, the motion light on C was on again.

She did not want to look.

She looked.

214’s lock was different—a silver one, Harbor rental style, which meant day crew had cut Okonkwo’s old lock after a missed payment or a transfer. The unit should have been empty or re-rented. The system said *available* as of Friday. Available units were not supposed to have light.

Leah’s key worked. The roll-up went up on empty concrete and a smell of wet cardboard that was only weather. No mattress. No Jamie. A single sock in the corner, grey, too small for the Jamie she remembered. Maybe not related. Units collected ghosts that did not know each other.

She bagged the sock because leaving it felt like littering inside a geometry problem. In the office she almost threw it away. She put it in the lost-and-found bin with a mitten from July and a pair of sunglasses missing one arm.

The regional number waited on the memo. She did not call. There was nothing to report except a sock and a feeling, and call centers were not staffed for feelings.


## 11

There was a week in late October when the gate keypad failed every third try and corporate sent a technician who smelled like cigarettes and patience. He replaced a board, tested the code, charged something to a cost center Leah would never see. While he worked, he told her about a facility in another state where someone had built a plywood room inside a twenty-by-twenty and lived there a year before a fire inspection. “Ended bad,” he said, without offering how.

“How bad,” Leah asked, against her better interest.

“Smoke. Nobody dead. Still.”

After he left she walked every row in daylight for once, looking at doors the way the inspection would. Most units were just units. One had a soft thump that turned out to be a trapped pigeon; she propped the door and it exploded into the lane like a grey insult. She laughed then, unexpected, and a customer with a hand truck gave her a look.

That same week Jamie’s number was still dark. Leah almost asked the shelter downtown if they had seen anyone matching a description and then imagined the form and the follow-up and the way “almost helped” becomes a file. She bought extra granola bars for the office drawer and told herself they were for her.


## 12

Mark called in November because their mother had fallen in the kitchen and insisted it was “just balance.” ER, then home with a wrist brace and a fury at being seen. Leah took two day shifts off and sat in the living room of the house she had grown up in while her mother watched a cooking show with the sound off.

“You look thin,” her mother said.

“You look braced.”

“Don’t be funny.”

Leah made tea in the kettle that still whistled in the key that meant childhood. On the fridge, under a magnet shaped like a lemon, a photo of June at a birthday. No photo of Leah’s work. What would it be—a roll-up door?

“Mark says you keep odd hours,” her mother said.

“Storage.”

“I know storage. I mean the face you make.”

Leah drank tea. “Someone was sleeping in a unit for a while. Kid. I didn’t call when I should have. Then I did other things that were also not procedure.”

Her mother was quiet in a way that used to mean anger and now meant effort. “Are they safe.”

“I don’t know.”

“Do you want to know.”

Leah watched the cooking show chef chop an onion into identical pieces. “Sometimes. Not enough to hunt.”

Her mother nodded once, as if that answer had a category. “Your father would have called the first night.”

“Dad called the HOA on Christmas lights.”

“That’s not the same.”

“It is a little the same.”

They left it there. The wrist brace scratched on the sofa arm. Leah washed the mugs and drove to Harbor for night shift with the radio off.


## 13

In December the company installed one new camera on bay C, finally. The image was sharp enough to see moths. Leah hated it a little. Clarity is a kind of obligation.

Two nights before Christmas a sedan rolled to the keypad at 11:40 and failed the code. The driver leaned out into the camera light: Jamie, older in the face by a margin you only see if you have been looking for it. Passenger seat empty. Jamie stared at the keypad, then at the camera, then backed out and left.

Leah’s hand was on the gate override for three full seconds. She took it off. The sedan’s taillights shrank toward the highway. She wrote nothing on a sticky note. She opened the log and typed: *11:40 failed gate attempt — no entry — no plate captured.*

True enough. Incomplete on purpose.


## 14

January. New year emails from corporate with fireworks GIFs that made the office computer wheeze. Leah enrolled in a community college class on Mondays—Intro to GIS—because maps felt cleaner than people and she needed a reason to be awake in daylight. The instructor wore sweaters and said “spatial joins” like a blessing. Leah sat in the back and took notes that looked like fence diagrams.

After class she sometimes drove past the bus stop by the tire place. Once there was a person in a hoodie who was not Jamie. Once there was no one. She stopped making the drive a ritual when she noticed she was disappointed by absence, which was a different problem than responsibility.


## 15

Spring. Okonkwo’s unit—re-rented to someone else months ago—came up in a boring way: the new tenant found a library card in a wall gap behind a shelf bracket and turned it in. The name on the card was not Jamie. Leah put it in lost-and-found next to the grey sock and felt the bin becoming a museum of almosts.

Anita retired. The replacement was named Chris and asked good questions and believed memos. Leah liked Chris and also missed the era of shared shrugging. Progress is like that.


## 16

On a night in April the motion light did its old dance and the new camera showed a cat. Leah laughed alone in the office, short, real. She walked the row anyway because legs need rounds. 214 was someone else’s junk now—labeled boxes, a bike frame, a life in cardboard. The silver lock shone. She did not touch it.

At the end of the lane she stood where the ditch water moved under the road grate and smelled less like oil than it had in August. A truck passed on the frontage road and threw light across the chain link. For a second the whole facility looked temporary, which it always had been.

Her phone buzzed. Unknown number.

*got a job washing dishes. room with a coworker. not a unit.*

No name. No hello.

Leah typed *good* and deleted it. Typed *glad* and deleted it. Sent: *ok. don’t use gate codes off posts.*

A minute later: *lol yes boss*

She put the phone face down. The camera on C showed empty asphalt and a moth. She finished her shift without a sticky note, which felt like a small unauthorized peace.


## 17

In May, Mark asked her to help move a dresser for June’s room. June was eight and wanted the dresser under the window “for looking out while choosing socks.” Leah lifted, Mark steered, they scraped a doorway anyway. June supervised with the authority of someone who owns the future.

After, on the porch with water, Mark said, “Mom told me you had a storage kid.”

“Of course she did.”

“You okay with how it went.”

Leah watched a neighbor water plants that were already dead. “I didn’t do it clean. I didn’t do it by the book. The kid is washing dishes and has a room. I didn’t make that happen. I also didn’t make a file that followed them around.”

“That’s not nothing.”

“It’s also not a system.”

Mark nodded. He had slept in a car and come out the other side into an apartment and a kid and a job that exhausted him. Systems had not saved him either. Sisters and time and luck and a garage full of tools had done pieces of it.

“You want dinner,” he said.

“Yeah.”


## 18

Harbor Self-Storage got a new paint job on the front sign in June. HARBOR in blue that hurt to look at at noon. Leah started day shifts twice a week for the GIS class schedule. Day customers were as annoying as she remembered and also occasionally kind in small ways—a man who brought donuts because his unit sale had gone well; a woman who apologized three times for crying over her dead husband’s fishing gear.

Leah still walked bay C when she closed. Habit. The camera saw her and she waved once, sarcastic, then felt silly and stopped.

She never saw Jamie at the gate again. She did get one more text in August, a photo of a kitchen sink stacked with plates and a hand in a rubber glove giving a thumbs-up that might have been ironic. Leah hearted it and did not reply. Some threads should thin out without a speech.


## 19

In late summer there was a heat wave that made the roll-up doors too hot to touch after three. Leah worked a double when Chris called out sick. The office fan clicked on every revolution like it was counting. A couple from out of state argued over whether their dead aunt’s china set needed climate control. Leah sold them a bigger unit and did not say that china breaks in any climate if you drop it.

At 9 p.m. a man asked for bolt cutters for his own lock because he had lost the key and his divorce papers were inside and the hearing was Thursday. Leah walked him through the authorized cut procedure, watched him sign, watched the day-lock go on after. His hands shook on the pen. She offered water. He said no. She offered water again; he took it. Not a story. Just Tuesday.

Walking bay C on that double, she stopped at 214 without meaning to. The new tenant’s bike frame still leaned inside the gap of a poorly closed door—she pushed the roll-up down until it latched. Small fixes. The kind that do not get you a memo.


## 20

Years do not resolve. They stack.

On an October night much later—same jacket, better boots—Leah trained a new night hire named Sam who asked, “What if someone’s living in a unit?”

Leah said the memo answer first: call regional, do not engage alone, document. Then she said, “And use your head. The memo doesn’t know the difference between a raccoon and a person who has run out of couches.”

“Which do I follow.”

“Both, until you can’t,” Leah said. “Then you choose and you live with the choosing. Write down what you did. Not for them. For you.”

Sam looked uneasy, which was appropriate. Leah showed them the camera board and the sticky notes and the ibuprofen drawer. At 1:07 nothing happened. At 1:40 they walked the rows under lights that worked better than they used to. Unit 214 was locked, quiet, ordinary.

Leah did not tell Sam about oatmeal or forty dollars or a sock in lost-and-found that she had eventually thrown away during a spring clean because bins cannot be museums forever. She kept those details where unfinished lines go—not erased, not public.

On the drive home the sky was starting to pale over the carpet warehouse. She passed the bus stop by the tire place. Empty bench. Paper cup on its side. She did not stop.

At her apartment she put her keys in the bowl and washed her hands longer than soap required. The water ran cold then warm. Outside, a garbage truck reversed with that steady warning beep. Inside, nothing arranged itself into a neat account of what she should have done at 1:07 on a night that was already years back. She ate cereal standing up. She checked the GIS homework on her laptop and fixed a projection error that had nothing to do with storage and felt, briefly, like a problem with a correct answer.

Sleep came in pieces. Near dawn she dreamed the motion light stayed on and when she opened 214 it was only cardboard and dust, and she was relieved and oddly disappointed in the same breath. She woke to her alarm and the ordinary work of getting dressed.

On the way to the car she passed her neighbor’s recycling, overflowing with Amazon boxes. She did not take a photo. She did not make a metaphor. She drove to Harbor with the window down, air still cool, and punched in for another stretch of hours among other people’s paused lives—boats, sofas, tax files, the occasional secret—while the ditch under the frontage road moved along without asking anyone to choose.
